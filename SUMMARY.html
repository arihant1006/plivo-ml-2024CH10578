Objective

Improve a deliberately mediocre GPT-style byte-level language model as much as possible under hard caps: 2,000 optimizer steps, 2,000,000 parameters, pure PyTorch/NumPy/stdlib, CPU only, no pretrained weights, trained solely on the provided mixed English+Hindi corpus (~7.3MB, ~14% Devanagari by character count). Scored via bits-per-byte (bpb) on held-out text using the official, unmodified evaluate.py sliding-window scorer.

Key Findings

  • The model was optimization-limited, not capacity- or regularization-limited. Learning rate alone (Run 4) produced a far larger gain (-0.0876 bpb) than anything else tested. Both weight tying (Runs 2–3) and a ~48% capacity increase (Run 6) made results worse, which is the clearest evidence that more parameters or more regularization were not the bottleneck at this step count — more/better-timed learning signal was.
  • Weight tying's usual benefit needs steps it doesn't have here. Forcing one matrix to serve both the input-embedding and output-classification roles is a well-established technique for longer training runs, but at 2000 steps it doesn't have time to settle into a good shared representation, and actively hurt convergence in isolation (Run 3 was the single worst result across all 7 runs).
  • Mapping the LR region before adding a scheduler paid off. Rather than reaching for warmup+cosine on convention, the LR was bracketed with plain constant-LR probes first (3e-4 too low, 1e-3 good, 2e-3 decisively unstable — a real ~700-step mid-run plateau, not noise). Only after that evidence existed was a schedule introduced, and it was decay-only — a mid-run analysis showed warmup wasn't justified here since the constant-1e-3 run was stable from step 1 with no early instability to protect against.
  • Hyperparameters are coupled, not independent (Run 8). A GPT-2-style tighter init (std=0.02 + residual-branch scaling) is a well-established technique, but tested against our already-tuned LR schedule it made results worse — most likely because init scale and learning rate jointly determine effective step size, and the LR schedule had been tuned specifically for the original std=0.05 init. This was correctly rejected as "this init doesn't work with this LR," not "this init is bad," given no time remained for a joint re-tune.
  • The BPE tokenizer (Run 10) was the single largest win of the session. The byte-level tokenizer tripled the token cost of Devanagari text (each Devanagari character is 3 UTF-8 bytes), affecting ~14% of train and ~21% of the held-out eval text. A from-scratch BPE tokenizer (pure Python/NumPy/stdlib, 500 merges, vocab 256→756) compressed this to 2.6-3.7 bytes/token, giving the model roughly 2.5x more real text per fixed 128-token context window. Given the hard, disqualifying round-trip-correctness requirement, this was verified explicitly on the full training corpus, the full dev_eval.txt, and out-of-distribution text (emoji, other scripts) before any model training began — the highest-ceiling lever, attempted deliberately rather than avoided, once real time budget allowed for proper verification.
  • SwiGLU (Run 9) was the second-largest win. Swapping the standard 4x-GELU MLP for a gated SwiGLU MLP, at essentially matched parameter count, gave a substantial improvement — multiplicative gating appears to extract more useful capacity per parameter than a plain wider projection, at this scale and step budget.

Human vs. AI Contribution

This was a human-directed, AI-executed process with an unusual extra step: a second AI (consulted by the human as an independent reviewer) was used throughout to critique the plan, and its suggestions were evaluated critically rather than adopted automatically — several were accepted, several were pushed back on with specific technical counter-arguments.

AI (Claude): code changes, running experiments, parameter/timing derivations, RUNLOG documentation Human: strategy, experiment sequencing, critical review, go/no-go calls, time management
  • Human decisions that changed the outcome: insisting on single-variable isolation instead of trusting a first bundled "hygiene" run at face value; explicitly rejecting premature jumps to a scheduler before the LR region was empirically mapped; catching an imprecise claim ("warmup can't hurt because it starts near zero") and forcing a mechanism-level correction; initially deprioritizing BPE as too risky given the clock, then explicitly overriding that caution once real time budget was confirmed and pushing to attempt it anyway rather than settling for the safer stopping point — a call that produced the single largest win of the session; and hard time-boxing the whole process against a real, non-negotiable submission deadline throughout.
  • AI contributions: repository/assignment analysis, all code edits (model.py, train.py), running and monitoring all 7 training + evaluation runs, deriving exact parameter-count formulas and verifying them against the real model rather than estimating, diagnosing *why* each failed run failed (not just that it failed), and maintaining RUNLOG.md/NOTES.md/this document throughout rather than after the fact.
  • Second-AI suggestions accepted: map the constant-LR region before introducing a scheduler; prioritize capacity over a scheduler once LR was resolved; keep RUNLOG conclusions appropriately hedged rather than over-certain.
  • Second-AI suggestions pushed back on or revised: an unverifiable claim about cosine schedules "losing" in an unrelated prior context was treated as anecdote, not evidence; "spend the unused parameter budget" was treated as a hypothesis to test rather than a free win (it was tested — Run 6 — and failed); and the original warmup justification was revised once the timing of Run 5's plateau (mid-run, not at initialization) showed warmup wasn't the right mechanism.
